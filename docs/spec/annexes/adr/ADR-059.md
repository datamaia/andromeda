### ADR-059 — Resilience mechanisms: class-keyed retries and a per-provider circuit breaker in the router

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-07-11
- Deciders: Andromeda specification team
- Components: Provider Router, adapters, Task Scheduler
- Related requirements: FR-PROV-040, FR-PROV-041, FR-PROV-050

#### Context

Provider failures are routine: rate limits, timeouts, transient 5xx, dead local servers.
Volume 3 places retry/fallback *policy* above adapters in the Provider Router; ADR-023 fixes
bounded concurrency and backpressure; ADR-016 fixes error identity. The chapter 06
normalization gives every failure a stable class.

#### Problem

Which resilience mechanisms exist, where do they live, and what keys their decisions —
per-adapter ad-hoc logic, SDK-inherited retry stacks, or router-owned mechanisms keyed to
normalized classes?

#### Forces and constraints

- Uniformity across nineteen adapters (ADR-019 rejected heterogeneous SDK retry stacks).
- Retry storms amplify outages; backoff must be jittered and bounded.
- Failing fast locally protects runs and saturated providers alike.
- Provider connection states are frozen entity vocabulary (Volume 2); mechanisms must
  drive, not duplicate, that machine (full machine: Volume 5, chapter 11).
- Retries can duplicate token spend; spend must stay visible (INV-COST rules).

#### Alternatives considered

1. **Per-adapter retry/breaker logic** — rejected: nineteen divergent behaviors; ADR-019
   uniformity lost; policy leaks into mechanism.
2. **SDK-provided retries where SDKs are adopted** — rejected as policy source: SDK retry
   behavior varies and hides attempts from accounting; adapters MUST disable SDK-internal
   retries where SDKs are adopted.
3. **Hedged/parallel duplicate requests** — rejected for inference: duplicate token spend
   by design contradicts cost transparency; latency hedging is unjustifiable at current
   budgets.
4. **Router-owned retries and per-provider breaker keyed to normalized classes** — chosen.

#### Decision

The Provider Router owns: (a) retry policy — jittered exponential backoff, defaults
`max_attempts` 3 / base 500 ms / ×2 / cap 10 s, provider retry-delay signals honored capped
at 60 s, eligibility solely from the chapter 06 retryability matrix, streaming retries only
before first delivery; (b) one circuit breaker per configured provider — rolling-window trip
guards (5 consecutive or ≥ 50% of ≥ 10 samples in 60 s), open durations 30 s doubling to
600 s, single half-open probe, in-memory state resetting to closed on restart; (c) local
pacing per provider (`max_concurrent_requests` 4, optional `requests_per_minute`). All
values are per-provider configuration keys.

#### Rationale

Centralizing policy in the router makes resilience uniform, testable with fake clocks, and
visible (retry events, attempt-numbered Cost Records); keying on normalized classes keeps
provider-specific judgment inside adapters' error mapping where it belongs.

#### Positive consequences

- One resilience behavior to learn, configure, and test.
- Breaker rejections (E-PROV-015) cost zero wire traffic and give routing truthful signals.
- Retry spend is first-class accounting data.

#### Negative consequences

- Router complexity concentrates; its tests carry the burden.
- Conservative defaults may under-utilize generous providers until tuned.

#### Risks

- Breaker flapping under intermittent failure; mitigation: doubling open durations,
  ratio-with-minimum-samples guard.
- Misclassified retryability amplifies failures; mitigation: matrix is normative and
  fault-injection covers every class (NFR-PROV-004).

#### Reversal plan

Mechanisms are router-internal; swapping algorithms (adaptive retry budgets, alternative
breaker designs) changes no port, adapter, or consumer surface and lands as a minor-release
change with config-key deprecations per Volume 10 rules.

#### Review conditions

- Review defaults against Volume 12 benchmark data at each phase gate.
- Review the no-hedging stance if a latency-critical, zero-marginal-cost path emerges
  (e.g., local models where duplicate spend is compute, not money).
