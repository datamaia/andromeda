### ADR-160 — Reference machines, reference datasets, and measurement methodology for performance verification

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-07-11
- Deciders: Andromeda specification team
- Components: benchmark harness, CI pipelines, all measured components
- Related requirements: Volume 12 NFR-PERF set; Volume 1, chapter 06 reference conditions

#### Context

Volume 1 defines product-level reference conditions (an Apple Silicon laptop with 16 GB, a
4 vCPU / 8 GB Linux VM, a 5,000-file reference repository) and assigns Volume 12 the formal
test-environment definitions. Twenty-plus latency and resource targets are meaningless
without a fixed machine, dataset, and percentile discipline: "≤ 50 ms p95" on unstated
hardware over an unstated corpus verifies nothing.

#### Problem

Which concrete environments, datasets, and measurement rules anchor every performance NFR so
that results are reproducible across contributors, CI, and years of releases?

#### Forces and constraints

- Volume 1's reference conditions are binding floors; Volume 12 may tighten, not weaken.
- Contributors must be able to run the suite on their own machines and know whether their
  numbers are comparable.
- Large fixture repositories cannot be committed to the monorepo; datasets must be
  generated, not stored.
- Hardware ages: canonical machines must be equivalence-classed, not brand-worshipped.
- Provider inference time is outside Andromeda's control and must never pollute Andromeda's
  budgets.

#### Alternatives considered

1. **Abstract hardware classes only** ("a modern laptop") — rejected: unverifiable, invites
   drift, and fails the Volume 0 ban on unverifiable normative statements.
2. **A single reference machine** — rejected: macOS and Linux are both Tier 1; a
   single-machine anchor would leave one Tier 1 platform's budgets unproven (SM-17 spirit).
3. **Committed fixture repositories** — rejected: hundreds of megabytes of dead weight in
   the monorepo, licensing questions for realistic content, and no path to DS-L scale.
4. **Percentiles over pooled cross-machine samples** — rejected: pooling hides
   machine-class effects; per-machine populations are the honest unit.

#### Decision

- Two reference machines: **RM-1** (canonical: MacBook Air M2, 8-core, 16 GB, 512 GB NVMe)
  and **RM-2** (canonical: 4 vCPU / 8 GB x86_64 VM, ≥ 500 MB/s SSD), each with a ±10%
  calibration equivalence rule on single-core CPU and sequential SSD read scores.
- Five deterministic datasets (DS-M, DS-L, DS-F, DS-MEM, DS-SOAK) produced by a seeded
  fixture generator versioned with the product.
- Methodology fixed in Volume 12, chapter 01: percentile populations (≥ 50 operation
  iterations, ≥ 1,000 micro-iterations), cold/warm definitions, provider isolation via mock
  provider, main-process-only resource accounting, monotonic clocks, fingerprinted results.
- Unless stated per-machine, targets and thresholds bind on both machines.

#### Rationale

Two machines cover both Tier 1 platform families at realistic consumer and CI-class
capability; the equivalence rule keeps the anchor stable as specific models leave the
market. Seeded generation gives arbitrarily large, license-clean, byte-reproducible corpora.
The methodology rules exist so every NFR's numbers mean the same thing in 2026 and in 2029.

#### Positive consequences

- Every NFR in Volume 12 is mechanically verifiable and reproducible.
- Contributor machines can self-classify via calibration; comparable results are labeled.
- Dataset scale (DS-L, DS-F) is available without repository bloat.

#### Negative consequences

- Maintaining calibration tooling and the fixture generator is real engineering work.
- Canonical-machine aging requires periodic recalibration decisions (review condition).
- Two-machine gating doubles benchmark CI time for gated runs.

#### Risks

- Equivalence rule too loose or too tight for future hardware (mitigation: review at each
  phase gate against calibration telemetry); generator realism drift versus real-world
  repositories (mitigation: periodic comparison of operation profiles against volunteer
  real-repo traces).

#### Reversal plan

Machines and datasets are named in exactly one chapter (Volume 12, chapter 01) and consumed
by reference; re-anchoring to new canonical machines or datasets is a MINOR document change
plus an explicit baseline reset in the benchmark store, with both result lines kept during
one overlap release.

#### Review conditions

- A canonical machine leaves practical availability, or calibration failure rates exceed
  10% of CI runs.
- Windows Tier 1 support (v2 candidate) requires an RM-3 definition.
- Volume 1 reference conditions change through the change procedure.
