### ADR-051 — Durable workflow timers: persisted wall-clock deadlines with a bounded sweep

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-07-11
- Deciders: Andromeda specification team (Volume 4 authoring)
- Components: Workflow Engine, Persistence Layer, Task Scheduler
- Related requirements: FR-WF-010; FR-WF-004, FR-WF-006

#### Context

Workflow correctness depends on timeouts: step timeouts, gate expiries, and run deadlines
(Volume 4 chapter 07). Workflow Runs outlive processes — they pause, the machine sleeps, the
process restarts. In-memory timers (Go `time.Timer`) die with the process and interact
platform-specifically with system sleep. The Approval entity already carries `expires_at`
(Volume 2); workflows need the enforcement mechanism.

#### Problem

How are workflow deadlines represented and enforced so that no deadline is ever lost across
process restarts, pauses, crashes, and system sleep, with a bounded and testable firing
latency?

#### Forces and constraints

- Deadlines must survive anything the run survives — persistence is the only durable medium.
- Firing must be exactly-once per deadline and must lose races gracefully (a grant recorded
  just before expiry wins).
- Monotonic clocks differ across platforms in whether sleep time counts; wall clocks can
  step.
- A high-frequency polling loop wastes the `background` pool; a purely event-driven design
  loses deadlines with the process.

#### Alternatives considered

1. **In-memory timers only** — rejected: deadlines vanish on crash/restart; gate expiries
   silently stop being enforced — a safety-model hole.
2. **OS-level scheduling (cron, launchd, systemd timers)** — rejected: violates the bounded
   process model (FR-ARCH-005, no daemons), is platform-divergent, and cannot participate in
   transactional guards.
3. **Database-poll-only (no in-memory schedule)** — workable but makes typical firing
   latency equal the poll interval; retained as the fallback layer, not the whole design.

#### Decision

Every workflow deadline is persisted as an **absolute UTC wall-clock instant** in the same
transaction as the transition that arms it; the persisted deadline is authoritative. An
in-memory schedule provides low-latency firing; a **sweep over persisted deadlines of live
runs at least every 30 seconds** bounds worst-case firing latency to deadline + 30 s
regardless of process restarts or sleep. Firing is a guarded, transactional, idempotent
transition; recovery re-arms from persisted deadlines and fires past ones immediately in
deadline order. Pause suspends step timers by persisting remaining budget; gate and run
deadlines keep running.

#### Rationale

Persist-first makes losing a deadline impossible without losing the database; the two-layer
design (schedule + sweep) gives interactive-grade latency in the common case and a provable
bound in every case; wall-clock authority makes the contract identical across platforms
while monotonic behavior remains a mere optimization concern.

#### Positive consequences

- Gate expiry enforcement — a safety property — holds across crashes and sleep.
- The 30-second bound is directly testable (kill/restart fixtures).
- Guarded firing makes grant-vs-expiry races deterministic.

#### Negative consequences

- Every armed deadline costs a persisted write; timer-heavy definitions add hot-path load.
- Sub-second timeout precision is out of scope; workflow timeouts are minutes-scale by
  design.

#### Risks

- Wall-clock steps (NTP, manual changes) shift firing by the step size; mitigation: sweeps
  re-evaluate continuously, and deadlines only delay, never disappear.
- Sweep query cost grows with live-run count; mitigation: indexed deadline columns and the
  Volume 12 live-run budgets.

#### Reversal plan

The sweep interval and the in-memory layer are internal; replacing either (e.g., with a
future SQLite-notify mechanism) preserves the persisted-deadline contract and the
firing-latency bound as the stable interface.

#### Review conditions

- Re-evaluate the 30-second bound if Volume 12 benchmarks show gate-expiry latency
  materially affecting approval UX.
- Resolve V4B-OQ-1 (monotonic clock behavior across sleep per Tier 1 platform) during Beta
  implementation; tighten the optimization-path documentation accordingly.
