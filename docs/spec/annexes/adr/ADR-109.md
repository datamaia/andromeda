### ADR-109 — Mouse support as enhancement-only, with guaranteed native-selection escape hatches

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-07-11
- Deciders: Andromeda specification team
- Components: TUI
- Related requirements: FR-TUI-005, FR-TUI-003; `tui.mouse` configuration key

#### Context

Terminal mouse reporting lets a full-screen application receive clicks, drags, and wheel
events — but enabling it takes over gestures the terminal otherwise handles, most
importantly click-drag text selection and the platform clipboard flow built on it.
Support and behavior vary across emulators, multiplexers, and SSH hops. Meanwhile the
TUI's primary audience works keyboard-first, and accessibility and automation
(teatest/v2 scripts, CI) cannot assume a pointer.

#### Problem

Decide the role of the mouse in the TUI: which interactions it drives, how it coexists
with native text selection and copying, and what happens where mouse reporting is absent
or broken.

#### Forces and constraints

- Keyboard parity is non-negotiable: golden tests, SSH-from-anywhere usage, and
  accessibility all require every action to be keyboard-reachable.
- Text selection/copy is a daily engineering operation (copying output, IDs, paths);
  breaking it silently is a top-tier annoyance in terminal apps that enable mouse mode.
- Wheel scrolling and click-to-focus are genuinely faster for transcript and list
  navigation and cost little.
- Approval prompts must not be decidable by a stray click buffered before the prompt
  appeared (same discipline as typed-ahead keys, FR-TUI-003).
- Terminals commonly bypass application mouse mode while a modifier is held, restoring
  native selection — a convention, not a standard.

#### Alternatives considered

1. **Enhancement-only mouse with selection escape hatches (selected).** Mouse drives
   focus, selection, activation, wheel scroll, and affordance clicks; nothing is
   mouse-only; two documented escape hatches for native selection.
2. **No mouse support at all** — simplest and selection-safe. Rejected: wheel scrolling
   in transcripts/logs and click-to-focus are high-frequency wins, mouse reporting is
   one message source in Bubble Tea (low cost), and modern terminal users expect wheel
   scroll to work in full-screen apps.
3. **Mouse-first interaction (buttons, drag-to-resize panels, context menus)** —
   rejected: duplicates every interaction in a second modality that CI and SSH cannot
   rely on, multiplies the golden matrix, and drags the design toward the GUI patterns
   Volume 1 excludes.
4. **Application-managed text selection (TUI implements its own selection and copies via
   OSC 52)** — rejected at MVP as the *replacement* for native selection: it must
   reimplement word/line/block semantics users already have, and OSC 52 clipboard
   support/size limits vary; per-element copy actions (chapter 11) plus native-selection
   escape hatches cover the need. Revisit for Beta as an additive convenience.

#### Decision

Mouse support is an **enhancement layer, never a requirement** (FR-TUI-005): left click
focuses and selects, double click activates, wheel scrolls the hovered panel, clicks on
labeled affordances trigger them. Every mouse action has a keyboard equivalent; no
information is hover-only. Native text selection is guaranteed by two escape hatches:
(1) the terminal's modifier-bypass convention where the terminal supports it, and (2)
`tui.mouse = false`, which disables mouse reporting entirely and is surfaced in help and
doctor output. Buffered clicks preceding an approval overlay are discarded exactly like
typed-ahead keys. Where mouse reporting is unavailable, absence is silent — no
degradation notices, no behavioral difference beyond the pointer doing nothing.

#### Rationale

The enhancement-only stance keeps the keyboard contract (tests, SSH, accessibility)
authoritative while banking the two cheap, high-value pointer wins. Making the selection
escape hatches part of the *contract* — documented, testable, one of them absolute
(`tui.mouse = false`) — addresses the classic full-screen-app complaint honestly instead
of assuming every terminal's modifier bypass works. Deferring app-managed selection
avoids reimplementing terminal functionality at MVP.

#### Positive consequences

- Wheel scrolling and click-to-focus work where terminals allow, at near-zero
  architectural cost (mouse events are ordinary messages).
- Text selection and copying always have a guaranteed path.
- The golden matrix stays keyboard-driven; mouse tests are a small additive suite.
- CI, SSH, and screen-reader-adjacent workflows lose nothing.

#### Negative consequences

- With `tui.mouse = true` (the default), plain click-drag selection is captured by the
  application in most terminals; users must learn the modifier bypass or toggle the key —
  a documented, but real, friction.
- No panel resizing or context menus by mouse; pointer-centric users get a deliberately
  limited experience.

#### Risks

- The modifier-bypass convention differs per terminal (and may be absent), making escape
  hatch (1) inconsistent; mitigated by hatch (2) being absolute and by chapter 12's
  matrix documenting per-terminal behavior.
- Wheel-event floods on high-resolution trackpads could pressure the render loop;
  mitigated by the same coalescing scheduler that bounds streaming repaints
  (FR-TUI-007).

#### Reversal plan

Removing mouse support is deleting one message handler class and the `tui.mouse` key
(with deprecation per configuration schema rules). Expanding toward app-managed
selection or richer pointer interaction is additive and would arrive as its own decision
with OSC 52 validation.

#### Review conditions

- Beta: revisit app-managed selection with OSC 52 copy as an additive convenience once
  the clipboard support matrix is validated (register open question territory).
- Terminal-matrix results showing the modifier bypass absent in a significant share of
  Tier 1-relevant terminals — would raise the visibility of the `tui.mouse` toggle in
  onboarding.
- User reports showing the default (`mouse = true`) generates selection complaints at a
  rate that argues for flipping the default.
